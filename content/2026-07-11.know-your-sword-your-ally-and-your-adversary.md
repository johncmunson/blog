---
title: Know Your Sword, Your Ally, and Your Adversary
description: A mental model for deciding what plane of abstraction to operate on when coding with LLMs.
---

A lot has been written about coding with LLMs. Agentic Engineering, if you will.

One of the biggest debates is about the plane of abstraction the human should operate on.

Should you review every line of code the agent generates? Should delegated tasks stay small, bounded, and easy to verify? Should you remain firmly in the weeds?

Or should you act more like a traffic controller? Should you largely remove yourself from the grind, trust the agent with long-horizon tasks, and focus on architecture, direction, and observational correctness?

As is so often the case, I don't think there is a one-size-fits-all answer. I also don't think these approaches are mutually exclusive. The right mode can change from project to project, task to task, and even hour to hour.

It's entirely contextual.

The mental model I've started using is to ask how well I know three things: **my Sword, my Ally, and my Adversary.**

# Your Sword: the tools and technology

Your Sword is the technology you're wielding: the language, framework, libraries, infrastructure, and development tools involved in the work.

How well do you know it?

If you're an expert in TypeScript, React, and the surrounding ecosystem, you can quickly recognize when generated code is fighting the framework, reaching for the wrong abstraction, or papering over a type error. You know where the sharp edges are. You know what idiomatic code looks like. You know which "clever" solutions will become maintenance problems six months from now.

If you're working in an unfamiliar stack, you lose that advantage. The code may look plausible without actually being good. You may be outsourcing not just the labor, but the underlying technical competence to the agent.

That isn't necessarily wrong. LLMs are incredibly useful for entering unfamiliar territory. But you should be honest with yourself about what is happening. If you don't know how to wield the Sword, you're in a poor position to notice when it's being used incorrectly.

Familiarity with the Sword also includes your ability to verify the result. Do you know how to run the test suite, inspect a query plan, read the logs, debug the runtime, or recognize a broken deployment? A stack with strong types, fast tests, good linting, and observable behavior gives both you and the agent a much tighter feedback loop than one with weak or nonexistent guardrails.

# Your Ally: the model and harness

Your Ally is the coding agent itself: the model plus the harness around it.

This is who you delegate to. He's your man to get shit done. He's on your side and on your team.

But can you trust him to get the job done right?

That trust shouldn't be based on vibes, benchmark charts, or the fact that a new model just dropped. It should come from experience. You need to learn where your Ally is strong, where he cuts corners, what kinds of instructions he follows reliably, and what kinds of tasks cause him to wander off into the woods.

A newer and more capable model is still a new Ally. Your old prompts may not produce the same behavior. The new model may be more literal, more eager, less proactive, more verbose, or more likely to make sweeping changes. You may need to revisit your instructions and retune the way you delegate.

The harness matters just as much. How well does it search the codebase? Does it run commands and inspect the results? Can it recover from errors? How does it manage context? What gets lost during compaction? Does it expose plans, diffs, checkpoints, or sub-agents? Does it tend to stop early, or can it reliably carry a task through implementation and verification?

"The model can do this" and "my agent can reliably do this in this repository" are very different claims.

Getting to know your Ally means building a calibrated sense of trust. Not blind trust, and not reflexive distrust. Calibrated trust.

# Your Adversary: the problem domain

Your Adversary is the problem you're trying to conquer: both the broader domain and the specific task at hand.

How well do you know the lay of the land? How well do you speak the native language?

You might be an expert in TypeScript but know very little about accounting and taxes. You may be able to evaluate the structure and quality of the implementation while completely missing that the business logic is wrong.

Or perhaps you're an ex-accountant who can precisely define the expected behavior but have limited experience in Python, the backend language your team decided to go with. You understand the Adversary, but not the Sword.

The specific task matters too. "Add a button to this existing page" and "redesign our authorization model" may use the same tech stack, but they are not the same class of problem. One has a narrow blast radius and obvious acceptance criteria. The other is loaded with hidden assumptions, security implications, and irreversible decisions.

Some adversaries are simply more dangerous than others. A slightly awkward internal admin page is not a funds-transfer system. A disposable prototype is not a database migration over production data. Even when your familiarity is identical, the cost of being wrong should change how closely you stay involved.

# Choosing your altitude

The plane you should operate on depends on how well you know all three.

When you know your Sword, trust your Ally, and understand your Adversary, you can operate at 10,000 feet. Delegate a broad outcome. Let the agent explore, implement, test, and iterate. Review the architecture, inspect the important boundaries, and judge whether the resulting system behaves correctly.

When one of the three is unfamiliar, drop your altitude.

- **Unfamiliar Sword:** Ask for smaller changes. Have the agent explain unfamiliar APIs and tradeoffs. Lean harder on documentation, tests, and external verification.
- **Unfamiliar Ally:** Start with bounded tasks. Watch how it behaves. Review its diffs. Learn its failure modes before handing it a long-horizon migration.
- **Unfamiliar Adversary:** Spend more time defining requirements and validating semantics. Bring in a domain expert. Don't confuse technically clean code with a correct solution.

When two or three are unfamiliar, you should probably be deep in the weeds. Or, reconsider whether you're equipped to supervise the work at all.

The inverse is also true. If all three are well known, insisting on reviewing every generated line may be an expensive form of ceremony. You're voluntarily giving up much of the leverage that the agent provides. In that context, tests, observability, architecture, and end-to-end behavior may be the more valuable things to review, allowing you to skim over ~90% of the diff.

This isn't a binary choice between "review everything" and "YOLO, let the agent cook." It's a continuously adjustable altitude.

You can delegate the initial implementation from 10,000 feet, descend to inspect a risky data migration, climb back up while the agent handles repetitive call-site changes, and then descend again to verify an important boundary. The appropriate plane changes as the work changes.

# Trust is local

The most important implication of this model is that confidence doesn't transfer automatically.

Being productive with one model doesn't mean you'll be equally productive with another. Knowing one harness doesn't mean you understand the behavior of the next. Expertise in one tech stack doesn't make unfamiliar code obvious. Success in one problem domain doesn't make you qualified to judge another.

Trust is local to a particular combination of Sword, Ally, and Adversary.

So before deciding how much autonomy to give an agent, ask:

1. **How well do I know the tools and technology involved?**
2. **How well do I know the model and harness doing the work?**
3. **How well do I understand the domain and the consequences of getting this task wrong?**

Your answers should determine your altitude.

The goal isn't to stay in the weeds forever. It also isn't to escape them as quickly as possible. The goal is to operate at the highest plane where your trust is actually justified and to know when the situation calls for coming back down.
